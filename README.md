## About This Project

This repository contains the research report, presentation slides, and survey questionnaire for a field survey study conducted on children's mental well-being in urban Pabna, Bangladesh.

The study explores how family environment, family structure, screen exposure, and selected socio-demographic factors are associated with the mental well-being of typically developing children and children with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD).

## Project Overview

Children’s mental well-being is influenced by the environment in which they grow up. This study examines whether children’s mental well-being is shaped more by the type of family they live in or by the quality of emotional support, safety, communication, and learning encouragement they receive at home.

The research focuses on children aged **6–12 years** and compares two groups:
* **Typically Developing** children
* **Children with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)**

---

## Study Information

| Item | Details |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Study Type** | Cross-sectional survey study |
| **Study Area** | Urban Pabna, Bangladesh |
| **Target Respondents** | Parents and primary caregivers |
| **Child Age Group** | 6–12 years |
| **Total Sample Size** | **339** |
| &nbsp;&nbsp;• *Typically Developing Children* | 254 |
| &nbsp;&nbsp;• *ASD Children* | 85 |
| **Main Outcome** | Children’s Mental Well-Being Score |

## Research Objectives

<details open>
<summary><b>Explore the core goals of this study (Click to collapse)</b></summary>
<br>

To understand how family environment and family structure are connected with children’s mental well-being among typically developing and ASD children in urban Pabna.

### 1. Family Background
* Explore the home environment in which children grow up.
* Identify the major family structures, including nuclear, joint, and single-parent families.

### 2. Child Mental Well-Being
* Assess children’s mental well-being based on parent-reported information.
* Compare the mental well-being of typically developing children and children with ASD.

### 3. Relationship Analysis
* Examine whether a supportive family environment is linked with better mental well-being.
* Compare children’s mental well-being across different family structures.
* Observe how family support, screen exposure, and selected background factors relate to children’s well-being.

</details>

## Family Environment and Mental Well-Being Results :

### Family Environment Indicators by Family Type

| Family Type              | Emotional Atmosphere | Argument Frequency | Emotional Safety | Parent-Child Quality Time | Family Learning Support |
| ------------------------ | -------------------: | -----------------: | ---------------: | ------------------------: | ----------------------: |
| **Joint Family**         |                3.333 |              3.657 |        **4.204** |                     3.574 |               **3.352** |
| **Nuclear Family**       |            **3.585** |          **3.774** |            4.157 |                 **3.590** |                   3.304 |
| **Single-Parent Family** |                2.714 |              3.429 |            2.929 |                     3.071 |                   2.857 |
| **Overall**              |                3.469 |              3.723 |            4.121 |                     3.563 |                   3.301 |

**Brief Insight:** Nuclear and joint families showed stronger family environment scores, while single-parent families had lower scores across most indicators.

---

### Item-wise Mental Well-Being Profile by Child Group

| Child Group              | Adapt Struggle | Behavior Problems | Difficult Behavior | Emotional Recovery | Express Needs | Focus | Social Interest | Worried Anxious |
| ------------------------ | -------------: | ----------------: | -----------------: | -----------------: | ------------: | ----: | --------------: | --------------: |
| **ASD**                  |          1.988 |             2.518 |              2.365 |              2.376 |         3.012 | 2.635 |           2.894 |           2.071 |
| **Typically Developing** |          3.610 |             4.350 |              3.709 |              3.835 |         4.602 | 3.843 |           4.331 |           3.705 |
| **Overall**              |          3.203 |             3.891 |              3.372 |              3.469 |         4.203 | 3.540 |           3.971 |           3.295 |

**Brief Insight:** Typically developing children scored higher than ASD children across all mental well-being items. Overall, the strongest item was **Express Needs**, while comparatively lower scores appeared in **Adapt Struggle** and **Worried Anxious**.

---

### Mental Development Score by Child Group

| Child Group              |  Mean |    SD | Min | Max |   N |
| ------------------------ | ----: | ----: | --: | --: | --: |
| **ASD**                  | 2.482 | 0.740 | 1.0 | 4.5 |  85 |
| **Typically Developing** | 3.998 | 0.582 | 2.0 | 5.0 | 254 |
| **Overall**              | 3.618 | 0.907 | 1.0 | 5.0 | 339 |

**Brief Insight:** Typically developing children showed a much higher average mental development score than ASD children. The overall mean score was **3.618**, indicating a moderate to good level of mental well-being in the full sample.

### Normality Test Results

| Variable                     |   N | Shapiro-Wilk Stat | Shapiro-Wilk p-value | K-S Stat | K-S p-value | Normal? |
| ---------------------------- | --: | ----------------: | -------------------: | -------: | ----------: | :-----: |
| **Mental Development Score** | 339 |            0.9398 |             1.68e-10 |   0.1272 |    3.06e-05 |    No   |
| **Family Environment Score** | 339 |            0.9508 |             3.23e-09 |   0.1046 |      0.0011 |    No   |
| **Child Age**                | 339 |            0.8636 |             1.09e-16 |   0.1814 |    3.11e-10 |    No   |

**Brief Insight:** All three variables had significant p-values, indicating that they did not follow a normal distribution. Therefore, non-parametric tests such as Spearman correlation, Mann-Whitney U, and Kruskal-Wallis were appropriate for further analysis.

### Chi Square Test



| Variables                         |     χ² | df | p-value | Cramér’s V | Effect    | Significant |
| --------------------------------- | -----: | -: | ------: | ---------: | --------- | :---------: |
| **Family Type × Child Group**     |  2.463 |  2 |   0.292 |      0.085 | Very weak |      No     |
| **Screen Time × Child Group**     | 21.724 |  4 |  < .001 |      0.253 | Weak      |     Yes     |
| **Screen Content × Child Group**  |  8.074 |  5 |   0.152 |      0.154 | Weak      |      No     |
| **Screen Use Time × Child Group** | 40.299 |  5 |  < .001 |      0.345 | Moderate  |     Yes     |

**Comment:**
The chi-square results show that **screen time** and **screen use time** were significantly associated with child group. The strongest association was found for **screen use time**, with a moderate effect size. This suggests that ASD and typically developing children differed more clearly in when they used screens. However, **family type** and **screen content** were not significantly associated with child group.

### Mann-Whitney U Test

The Mann-Whitney U test was used to compare numerical scores between two independent groups because the variables were not normally distributed.

| Comparison          | Score      |        U | p-value |      r | Effect    | Significant |
| ------------------- | ---------- | -------: | ------: | -----: | --------- | :---------: |
| **TD vs. ASD**      | Mental WB  | 20,241.0 |  < .001 | -0.875 | Strong    |     Yes     |
| **Male vs. Female** | Mental WB  | 12,535.0 |   0.388 |  0.056 | Very weak |      No     |
| **TD vs. ASD**      | Family Env | 14,233.5 |  < .001 | -0.319 | Moderate  |     Yes     |

**Comment:**
The results show a significant difference in **mental well-being** between typically developing children and children with ASD, with a strong effect size. Typically developing children had higher mental well-being scores than ASD children. A significant difference was also found in **family environment scores** between TD and ASD children, with a moderate effect size. However, mental well-being did not differ significantly between male and female children.

### Kruskal-Wallis Test

The Kruskal-Wallis test was used to compare numerical scores across three or more independent groups because the variables were not normally distributed.

| Grouping Variable     | Score Variable |      H | df | p-value |    ε² | Effect    | Significant |
| --------------------- | -------------- | -----: | -: | ------: | ----: | --------- | :---------: |
| **Family Type**       | Mental WB      |  3.245 |  2 |   0.197 | 0.004 | Very weak |      No     |
| **Family Type**       | Family Env     | 11.640 |  2 |   0.003 | 0.029 | Weak      |     Yes     |
| **Mother Education**  | Mental WB      | 45.888 |  7 |  < .001 | 0.117 | Moderate  |     Yes     |
| **Father Education**  | Mental WB      | 20.463 |  8 |   0.009 | 0.038 | Weak      |     Yes     |
| **Father Occupation** | Family Env     |  8.413 |  7 |   0.297 | 0.004 | Very weak |      No     |
| **Mother Occupation** | Family Env     | 11.558 |  4 |   0.021 | 0.023 | Weak      |     Yes     |
| **Screen Time**       | Mental WB      | 13.928 |  3 |   0.003 | 0.033 | Weak      |     Yes     |

**Comment:**
The results show that **mental well-being differed significantly by mother’s education, father’s education, and screen time**. Mother’s education showed the strongest effect, suggesting that maternal education may play an important role in children’s mental well-being. Family environment also differed significantly across family types, but the effect was weak. However, mental well-being did not differ significantly across family types, and father’s occupation was not significantly related to family environment.


## Multiple Regression Analysis

The multiple regression analysis was conducted to answer the key question:

**Does family environment still explain children’s mental well-being after controlling for family structure and other background factors?**

### Variable Selection for Modeling

| Variable Group                   | Variables                                                                                                                                                                  |
| -------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Outcome Variable**             | Mental Well-Being Score                                                                                                                                                    |
| **Selected Predictor Variables** | Family Environment Score, Family Type, Child Group, Child Age, Child Gender, Household Size, Screen Time, Father’s Education, Mother’s Education, Monthly Income           |
| **Excluded Variables**           | Primary Caregiver, School Status, Screen Content, Screen Use Time, Father’s Occupation, Mother’s Occupation, Family Environment Item Scores, Mental Well-Being Item Scores |

### Hierarchical Model Plan

| Model       | Variables Added                        | Purpose                         |
| ----------- | -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------- |
| **Model 1** | Child factors                          | Controls basic child profile    |
| **Model 2** | Model 1 + Family type + Household size | Tests family structure effect   |
| **Model 3** | Model 2 + Family Environment Score     | Tests family environment effect |
| **Model 4** | Model 3 + Screen Time                  | Adds screen exposure            |
| **Model 5** | Model 4 + Parental education + Income  | Final adjusted model            |

---

### Multiple Regression Model Fit

| Indicator         |  Value |
| ----------------- | -----: |
| **N**             |    339 |
| **R²**            |  0.685 |
| **Adjusted R²**   |  0.652 |
| **F-statistic**   | 20.753 |
| **Model p-value** | < .001 |

**Comment:**
The overall regression model was statistically significant. The model explained about **68.5%** of the variation in children’s Mental Well-Being Score, showing strong explanatory power.

---

### Significant Adjusted Predictors

| Predictor                          |      B | p-value |
| ---------------------------------- | -----: | ------: |
| **Family Environment Score**       |  0.414 |  < .001 |
| **Child Group: ASD**               | -1.204 |  < .001 |
| **Mother Education: HSC**          | -0.271 |   0.012 |
| **Mother Education: No schooling** | -0.623 |   0.006 |
| **Mother Education: Primary**      | -0.675 |  < .001 |
| **Mother Education: SSC**          | -0.295 |   0.013 |

**Comment:**
Family Environment Score was a significant positive predictor of children’s mental well-being. This means that children from better family environments tended to have higher mental well-being scores. ASD status was a strong negative predictor, showing that ASD children had lower mental well-being scores than typically developing children after adjustment. Mother’s education also remained an important predictor.

---

### Hierarchical Model Comparison

| Model       | Block Added                            |     R² | Adjusted R² |      ΔR² | p-value for Change | Decision                   |
| ----------- | -------------------------------------- | -----: | ----------: | -------: | -----------------: | -------------------------- |
| **Model 1** | Child factors                          | 0.5425 |      0.5384 | Baseline |           Baseline | Baseline model             |
| **Model 2** | Family structure and household context | 0.5452 |      0.5370 |   0.0027 |             0.5848 | No significant improvement |
| **Model 3** | Family environment                     | 0.6291 |      0.6212 |   0.0839 |             < .001 | Significant improvement    |
| **Model 4** | Screen exposure                        | 0.6455 |      0.6335 |   0.0164 |             0.0051 | Significant improvement    |
| **Model 5** | Socioeconomic background               | 0.6846 |      0.6516 |   0.0391 |             0.0175 | Significant improvement    |

**Comment:**
The hierarchical regression shows that adding **family structure** did not meaningfully improve the model. However, adding **Family Environment Score** produced a clear and significant improvement. This suggests that the quality of the family environment explains children’s mental well-being better than family structure alone.

## Binary Logistic Regression

Binary logistic regression was used to identify the factors associated with **high mental well-being** among children.

### Significant Predictors of High Mental Well-Being

| Predictor                     | Adjusted OR |         95% CI | p-value | Interpretation                                                                                                                                |
| ----------------------------- | ----------: | -------------: | ------: | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Family Environment Score**  |      5.8638 | 3.0461–11.2881 |  < .001 | Better family environment increased the odds of high mental well-being.                                                                       |
| **Child Group: ASD**          |      0.0219 |  0.0066–0.0722 |  < .001 | ASD children had much lower odds of high mental well-being than TD children.                                                                  |
| **Screen Time: 1–2 hours**    |      2.4013 |  1.0660–5.4089 |  0.0345 | Children with 1–2 hours of screen time had higher odds of high mental well-being than the reference group.                                    |
| **Mother Education: Primary** |      0.0624 |  0.0087–0.4458 |  0.0057 | Children whose mothers had primary education had lower odds of high mental well-being than those whose mothers had Master’s degree or higher. |

### Model Evaluation

| Accuracy | Precision | Recall | F1-score | ROC-AUC |
| -------: | --------: | -----: | -------: | ------: |
|   0.8201 |    0.7947 | 0.8728 |   0.8320 |  0.8931 |

**Comment:**
The logistic regression model showed good predictive performance, with an accuracy of **82.01%** and ROC-AUC of **0.8931**. Family environment was the strongest positive predictor of high mental well-being, while ASD status and lower maternal education were associated with lower odds of high mental well-being.

## Key Findings

* **Family environment** showed a positive relationship with children’s mental well-being.
* **Parent-child quality time** and **emotional safety** were among the strongest family-related factors.
* **Typically developing children** showed higher mental well-being scores than children with ASD.
* **Family structure alone** did not strongly explain children’s mental well-being after considering other factors.
* **Balanced and guided screen use** may be important for children's mental and behavioral development.
* **Mother’s education** appeared to be an important factor associated with children’s mental well-being.

> **💡 Core Dynamic** > Overall, the quality of care, emotional safety, and support inside the family appeared more important than family type alone.

### Main Conclusion
The study suggests that children's mental well-being is shaped more by **how the family functions** than by the family structure itself. 

A caring, calm, emotionally safe, and learning-friendly home environment may play an important role in improving children's mental well-being, especially among children with ASD.
